📖 What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where cybercriminals impersonate trusted individuals or organisations to trick victims into revealing sensitive information — such as passwords, credit card numbers, or login credentials.

⚡ Key Fact

According to the Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report, over 36% of all data breaches in recent years involved phishing. It remains the #1 initial attack vector used by threat actors globally.

🇳🇬 Nigeria-Specific Threat Landscape

In Nigeria, common phishing scenarios include fake EFCC/FIRS tax notices, fraudulent bank "account verification" emails, BVN update scams, and fake job offers. Attackers often impersonate GTBank, First Bank, UBA, and government agencies like INEC or JAMB.

🔎 How to Recognize a Phishing Attempt

Attackers are clever, but phishing emails and websites almost always leave behind clues. Learn to spot them:

📮 Suspicious sender address
support@paypa1.com instead of support@paypal.com
🚨 Urgency & fear tactics
"Your account will be suspended in 24 hours!"
🔗 Mismatched / suspicious URLs
Hover before clicking — the link shows a different domain
✍️ Grammar & spelling errors
Legitimate companies have professional copywriters
📎 Unexpected attachments
Especially .exe, .zip, .docm, .xlsm files
💳 Requests for sensitive data
Banks never ask for your password via email
🎁 Too-good-to-be-true offers
"You've won a ₦5,000,000 prize! Claim now."
🖼️ Generic greetings
"Dear Customer" instead of your actual name
🔒 No HTTPS / invalid certificate
Fake sites often lack valid SSL certificates

✅ The 5-Second Rule

Before clicking ANY link in an email, spend 5 seconds asking yourself: Was I expecting this? Does the sender make sense? Does the link match what I expect? If the answer to any is "no" — do not click.

🎭 Social Engineering Tactics

Phishing is a subset of social engineering — the art of psychologically manipulating people. Here are the core tactics attackers use:

1. Pretexting

The attacker fabricates a scenario (a "pretext") to gain trust. Example: Calling a staff member pretending to be IT support: "Hi, I'm James from the help desk. We're doing a security audit and need you to confirm your password."

2. Authority & Impersonation

Attackers impersonate authority figures — a bank, your CEO, EFCC, JAMB, or INEC — because people are conditioned to obey authority. An email from the "Managing Director" requesting an urgent wire transfer is a classic example (BEC — Business Email Compromise).

3. Scarcity & Urgency

Creating artificial time pressure disables rational thinking. "This offer expires in 2 hours" or "Your account will be suspended today" forces victims to act before they can verify.

4. Reciprocity

Giving something small (a gift, a discount, helpful information) to trigger a psychological obligation to give something back — like credentials.

5. Baiting

Leaving an infected USB drive in a car park or sending a "Free Movie Download" that is actually malware. Curiosity kills the cat — and compromises the network.

🛡️ Defend Against Social Engineering

  • ✔ Always verify requests through a separate, trusted channel — call the person back on a known number.
  • ✔ Slow down when you feel rushed — urgency is the attacker's best weapon.
  • ✔ Never provide sensitive info to someone who contacted you unsolicited.
  • ✔ Security policies exist for a reason — no legitimate request should require bypassing them.

🛡️ Best Practices to Stay Safe

For Individuals

  • ✔ Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on all accounts. Even if attackers get your password, they cannot log in without your second factor.
  • ✔ Use a password manager — it auto-fills only on the legitimate domain, blocking credential theft on fake sites.
  • ✔ Never click links in emails. Go directly to the website by typing the URL yourself.
  • ✔ Hover over links before clicking to preview the real destination URL.
  • ✔ Keep your OS, browser, and antivirus updated.
  • ✔ Report suspected phishing emails to your IT/security team — don't just delete them.
  • ✔ Be suspicious of unexpected emails, even from known contacts (their accounts may be compromised).

For Organisations

  • ✔ Deploy email filtering solutions (e.g., Proofpoint, Mimecast) to block phishing emails before they reach inboxes.
  • ✔ Implement DMARC, DKIM, and SPF DNS records to prevent domain spoofing.
  • ✔ Conduct regular phishing simulations and awareness training for all staff.
  • ✔ Establish a clear incident response plan for when phishing succeeds.
  • ✔ Use DNS filtering to block access to known malicious domains.

📲 What To Do If You're Phished

1. Change your password immediately on the real site.
2. Enable MFA if not already active.
3. Alert your bank/service provider.
4. Report to your organisation's IT team or CERT.
5. Monitor your accounts for unusual activity.
